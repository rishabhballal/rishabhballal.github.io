Geometric models

One naturally visualises a geometric structure in 3-dimensional space (R3). How then does the mathematician understand an n-dimensional structure in the absence of an ambient space? In the first step towards an intrinsic formulation that reflects the underlying geometry, the mathematician develops the notion of a manifold.


Bearing a remarkable analogy with the subject of geography, an n-dimensional manifold is defined by an atlas, which is a collection of charts. Each chart assigns coordinates to a region of the manifold, such that the atlas covers the manifold.


The simplest examples are some familiar 2-dimensional manifolds, as shown below. (For the code behind the models, refer: constructing 3D models using canvas in JavaScript.)

The Sphere

A representation of perhaps the most interesting symmetry in Nature, the sphere carves its way into most fields of physics. In geometry, it is the standard example of positive curvature, and is also simply-connected, which means that any closed curve on its surface can be continuously contracted to a point on the same curve.

The Hyperboloid

Contrary to the sphere, the hyperboloid is the standard example of negative curvature, and finds itself primarily amongst cosmological models. Depicted alongside is the one-sheeted version, which is connected unlike the two-sheeted hyperboloid.

The Torus

More popularly known as the donut, the torus is constructed by assigning a circle to each point of another circle. As any closed curve that wraps around the torus cannot be contracted to a point, the torus only differs from the sphere in that it is not simply-connected.

The Möbius Strip

Having only one edge and one surface, the Möbius strip is a non-orientable manifold; traversing the strip results in an inversion of axis orientation after each loop. One can model the Möbius strip by recognising its boundary as a closed curve on the torus.
